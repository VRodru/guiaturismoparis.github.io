Notre Dame Cathedral

The Gothic cathedral of Notre Dame is the seat of the Archdiocese of Paris. Dedicated to the Virgin Mary, it is located on the Cité Island, surrounded by the Seine River. Construction began in 1163 and was completed in 1345. In 1314, Jacques de Molay, Grand Master of the Knights Templar, was burned alive in front of the still-under-construction cathedral by King Philip IV.

During the French Revolution, in the 1790s, Notre Dame was desacralized, damaged, and used as a warehouse. In 1802, Napoleon Bonaparte returned it to the Church, and in 1804, he was crowned emperor in the cathedral, with Simón Bolívar among the attendees.

On April 15, 2019, a devastating fire caused severe damage. The roof and the central spire designed by Viollet-le-Duc were destroyed, and the rose windows were damaged. Despite the tragedy, the French people and the international community came together to restore the monument.

The western facade of Notre Dame features the Door of the Virgin, the Door of the Last Judgment, and the Door of Saint Anne. Above, the Gallery of Kings celebrates a victory of the Capetian dynasty.

One of the cathedral’s treasures is the Rose Window, built in 1220. It is the largest of its time, with stained glass depicting biblical and astronomical scenes, illuminating the interior of the cathedral.

The towers of Notre Dame, standing at 69 meters tall, are defensive elements and offer a panoramic view of Paris.

The restoration of Notre Dame, initiated after the fire, is a monumental effort to return the cathedral to its original splendor, celebrating the history and faith of generations.
