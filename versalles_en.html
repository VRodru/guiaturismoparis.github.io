Palace of Versailles

The Château de Versailles is a former royal residence built by King Louis XIV in Versailles, about 19 kilometers from Paris. The palace is owned by the French Republic and receives around 15 million visitors annually. Construction began in 1623 by Louis XIII, who built a simple hunting lodge on the site.

Louis XIV expanded the lodge, transforming it into a palace. Construction continued from 1661 until 1715, and in 1682, Louis XIV moved the court to Versailles, making it the de facto capital of France. This situation persisted with Kings Louis XV and Louis XVI, who made interior reforms.

In 1789, the royal family returned to Paris, and the palace was largely abandoned during the French Revolution, being stripped of its valuables. After the Revolution, Napoleon I used the palace as a summer residence between 1810 and 1814, restoring the Grand Trianon.

During the Bourbon Restoration, the palace was partially restored in the 1830s. A museum of French history was established inside, replacing the rooms in the south wing. In 1979, the Palace of Versailles and its park were designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Inside Versailles, you will find the Queen's Hamlet, a part of the Petit Trianon commissioned by Marie Antoinette in 1782. The queen sought to escape the rigidity of the court by creating a small paradise inspired by the ideas of Rousseau.

The village had an agricultural function, with a farm producing milk and eggs for the queen. At its center was a pond and several buildings, including wooden cottages, a mill, and the "Queen's House" near a stone bridge.

After the Revolution, the village was restored three times, the last being in the 1990s, driven by Pierre-André Lablaude. In 2006, it was opened to the public as part of the Domains of Marie Antoinette.
