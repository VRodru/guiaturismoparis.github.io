Basilica of Sacré Cœur de Montmartre

The Basilica of Sacré Cœur de Montmartre, also known as the Basilica of the National Vow, is a minor basilica and sanctuary for Eucharistic worship located on the Montmartre hill, in the 18th district of Paris, specifically in Clignancourt.

The construction of the basilica was approved in 1873 by the National Assembly, at the request of several notable Parisians and with the collaboration of the Archbishop of Paris. It was built as a tribute to the French victims of the Franco-Prussian War and was constructed in the context of restoring moral order and authority following the upheavals of the Paris Commune.

The goal of the basilica was also to serve as an expiatory temple, to atone for the suffering caused by the war and political events. Its construction symbolizes the restoration of faith and national unity after France's defeat in 1871.

Located 130 meters above sea level, the basilica dominates the city and is visible from various points in Paris thanks to its imposing 83-meter-high dome. This strategic location gives it exceptional visibility.

The Sacré Cœur is one of the main pilgrimage sites in Paris, receiving nearly 11 million visitors annually. It is the second most visited religious monument in the city, surpassed only by the Notre Dame Cathedral.

The basilica is a major tourist destination due to its architecture, which combines Romanesque and Byzantine styles. Its structure is topped with a dome, which offers an impressive panoramic view of the city of Paris.

Today, the Basilica of the Sacré Cœur is not only a place of worship but also a symbol of the Catholic faith in Paris and a testimony to the city's history.
