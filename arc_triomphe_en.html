Arc de Triomphe

Arc de Triomphe, built between 1806 and 1836 by order of Napoleon Bonaparte to commemorate the victory at the Battle of Austerlitz (1805), stands 49 meters tall and 45 meters wide. This iconic monument is located in the 8th arrondissement of Paris, on the Place Charles de Gaulle, also known as Place de l'Étoile (Star Square), which forms a massive roundabout with twelve avenues designed in the 19th century, creating a star-shaped pattern when viewed from the air.

The arch, in neoclassical style, was designed by architect Jean Chalgrin, who was inspired by Roman architecture, particularly the Arch of Titus in Rome. The structure has four enormous pillars that support its immense weight, decorated with reliefs depicting scenes of Napoleon's victories. Additionally, above the arch are 30 panels representing the most significant campaigns and battles of the Napoleonic era.

At the base of the Arc de Triomphe lies the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier from World War I, a tribute to those who fell during the conflict. On its surface, an inscription reads: “ICI REPOSE UN SOLDAT FRANÇAIS MORT POUR LA PATRIE, 1914-1918” (“Here rests a French soldier who died for the Fatherland, 1914-1918”), and an eternal flame, which is rekindled every day at 6:30 p.m. by associations of former soldiers or war victims.

This ritual has been maintained over the years as a symbol of respect and homage to those who died for France. Furthermore, the Arch becomes the center of the commemorations on November 11th, the date of the 1918 armistice between France and Germany, which ended World War I. On this date, thousands of Parisians and visitors gather to honor the fallen.

The Arc de Triomphe is not only a symbol of Napoleon's victory but also a monument that represents the sacrifice of French soldiers throughout history. Every detail of its architecture and every inscription on its walls tells the story of France’s struggle for freedom, independence, and national glory.

The view from the top of the arch is spectacular. From its observation platform, located 50 meters above ground, you can enjoy a unique panoramic view of Paris, with the Champs-Élysées stretching to the east and the city’s most iconic monuments, such as the Eiffel Tower and Sainte-Chapelle, visible in the distance. This makes it one of the most visited viewpoints in the city.

Additionally, the Arch has witnessed numerous historical events, such as military parades and popular demonstrations. Its strategic location, in one of the busiest squares in the city, makes it a living symbol of France's history, always present in the collective memory of the French people.
