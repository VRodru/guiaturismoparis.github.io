Louvre Museum

The Musée du Louvre is the national museum of France, dedicated to fine arts, archaeology, and decorative arts from before Impressionism. It is located in the former royal palace of the Louvre, covering 210,000 m² and housing around 300,000 works of art.

The Louvre is the most visited art museum in the world, known for its vast collection of masterpieces. Among them stands the Renaissance painting of Mona Lisa. In 2020, the museum selected the "24 must-see works," which include, in addition to the aforementioned painting, the Code of Hammurabi, The Seated Scribe, The Coronation of Napoleon, and Liberty Leading the People.

The building that houses the museum has its origins in the Louvre Castle, built in the 12th century. Over the centuries, it was expanded, particularly during the Renaissance. In the 17th century, the Palace of Versailles became the new royal residence, leaving the Louvre vacant.

During the 18th century, the Louvre began to house academies such as the French Academy. After the French Revolution, it became a center of art and science, and in 1793 it opened its doors as a museum.

In 1803, under the rule of Napoleon Bonaparte, the museum was renamed the Musée Napoléon. The Napoleonic conquests enriched the Louvre's collection as works from the invaded countries were seized.

The museum was connected to the Palace of the Tuileries until its destruction in 1870, during the Paris Commune. Since then, the Louvre has dominated the park that once occupied that space.

During the Nazi occupation, many works were evacuated for protection. In 1939, as the Second World War approached, works such as The Mona Lisa were moved to safe locations.
