Eiffel Tower

The Eiffel Tower, originally called Tour de 300 mètres, is 300 meters tall plus 30 meters for the antenna and has public access to the stairs only up to the second floor with 1,665 steps. The tower was built for the 1889 World's Fair and is the tallest structure in the city. It did not achieve true massive and sustained success until the 1960s, with the development of international tourism, and today it is the most visited tourist monument in the world, with 7.1 million visitors each year. At the edge of the first floor, one can observe the names of 72 personalities from the scientific world of the 18th and 19th centuries inscribed around the tower.

After fulfilling its role in the 1889 and 1900 World's Fairs, it was used for tests by the French army with communication antennas, and today it is used as a broadcaster for radio and television programs.

Learning a bit more, when World War I broke out in 1914, a receiver located on the tower intercepted enemy radio communications, seriously hindering the German advance on Paris, contributing to the Allied victory in the First Battle of the Marne.

After the German occupation of Paris in 1940 during World War II, the elevator cables were sabotaged by the French to prevent Hitler from viewing the fallen city from the top.

In August 1944, as the Allied Army was approaching Paris, Hitler ordered General Dietrich von Choltitz, military governor of Paris, to demolish the tower along with the entire city. Von Choltitz disobeyed the order. The tower, which had survived a fire caused by German troops, was used to communicate with the forces, first by the Wehrmacht and later by the Allies during the Liberation of Paris.

The tower received its 200,000,000th visitor on November 28, 2002, becoming the fifth most visited monument in France.
